# Probability of Detection (POD)

Here I include an excerpt from recent work in which I detail the steps of the a-hat vs a POD evaluation framework which I apply to in-situ defect detection of laser powder bed fusion (L-PBF).

A standard NDE process for assessing detection performance with relation to defect size is the POD method (U.S. Department of Defense, 2009). This approach posits an underlying mathematical relationship between the probability of detecting a defect and the defect size (e.g. a larger defect has a higher probability of detection), with the parameters of this relationship estimated via Generalised Linear Modelling. The result is a cumulative distribution function (CDF) that describes the probability of a model detecting a defect as a function of defect size. The standard NDE reference metric, known as **$a_{90/95}$** or the "reliably detected defect size", is then obtained from this CDF. It is the size that achieves a 90% probability of detection, with 95% confidence.

There are two possible styles of approach with POD, either through hit/miss data or **$\hat{a}$** vs. **$a$** (i.e. predicted versus actual size). The first method compares the sizes of defects that were detected (hits) with those that were not (misses) (ASTM, 2023). The latter method offers a more precise estimate of **$a_{90/95}$** as it incorporates information regarding detection signal strength, rather than binarising the data into either a hit or a miss (ASTM, 2021). Both methods fit a statistical model to the data, providing an estimate of the defect size that is likely to be detected. The **$\hat{a}$** vs. **$a$** method is the style of approach used in this work. The mathematics behind **$\hat{a}$** vs. **$a$** is quite technical, and typically, users of the framework would employ the well-known POD software that follows the handbook MIL-HDBK 1823 (U.S. Department of Defense, 2009), or use the ASTM E3023-21 standard (ASTM, 2021). For completeness, the method will be explained here. A flowchart of the key steps is shown in Figure 1.

Before analysis, the inference outputs require some pre-processing in order to determine the predicted defect size, **$\hat{a}$**, for each defect. The initial dataset of predictions comprises layer-wise probability maps that consist of 0.125×0.125 $mm^2$ pixels that are in the range 0…1, where typically they would only undergo thresholding (e.g., defect probability ≥ class prediction theshold) to enable binary classification. For this methodology, we are more interested with the model’s raw signals (i.e., pre-thresholding) as a pixel value of 0.99 shows a much stronger detection capability, and thus POD, than say a pixel value of 0.51. This requires some additional steps when processing each layer. To determine which pixels to include in the calculation of predicted defect size, a binary detection map was used, which was obtained by thresholding the defect probability map with the class prediction threshold. A union mask was then formed between this detection map and the ground truth defect mask. To prevent distant false-positive indications from inflating the measurement, the union mask was refined by retaining only those detected regions that were connected to the ground truth defect region. Here, 8-connectivity was adopted, such that pixels connected along edges or corners were considered part of the same region. The predicted area was then computed by summing the defect probability values within this refined ground-truth-connected union mask and multiplying the result by the area of one pixel (0.125×0.125 $mm^2$). This approach captures both the strength of the model’s correct detections while also considering how close the model was in cases where it failed to detect. The actual defective area for each layer is calculated using the CAD geometries. 

With values for both predicted (**$\hat{a}$**) and actual (**$a$**) defect sizes, POD analysis can begin. First, the relationship between **$\hat{a}$** and **$a$** must be inspected (Figure 1 – step 1). In this work, Cartesian **$\hat{a}$** and **$a$** were chosen as the model is trained to predict defect size, thus we expect a linear correlation, and this is confirmed when inspecting the relationship. Following that, a decision threshold (**$\hat{a}_{decision}$**) is selected (Figure 1 – step 2), where any predicted defective area that falls below the threshold is considered as defect-free material (i.e. a non-detection). Example data is shown in Figure 2, where the defect sizes **$a_{50}$** and **$a_{90/95}$** are shown, along with their corresponding bell curves that indicate the probability of the detection (i.e. probability of being greater than the decision threshold). While the **$a_{50}$** bell curve has its mean situated on the line defined by the estimated regression parameters, the **$a_{90/95}$** bell curve is instead situated on the lower 95% confidence bound (right-most) for the regression parameters, which is obtained using the Wald Method (U.S. Department of Defense, 2009; Wald, 1943). In this work, the decision threshold (**$\hat{a}_{decision}$**) was set at the calibrated class prediction threshold (**$t_{\gamma}$**) multiplied by the area of one pixel (e.g., if a model had a class prediction threshold of 0.5, then the predicted size decision threshold (**$\hat{a}_{decision}$**) would be 0.5×0.125×0.125 $mm^2$), so that any region with a raw signal greater than or equal to the class prediction threshold is treated as a detection.

Censoring of the data also takes place at this step. Censoring is important when the true signal exists but the measurement instrument or the detection model cannot resolve it below a certain threshold. U.S. Department of Defense (2009) recommends censoring the data in this way, otherwise the estimated regression fit is biased downwards and a less conservative estimate of **$a_{90/95}$** is obtained. In our system, there are some defective layers that correspond to a predicted area value of zero. This is deemed as a limitation of the model and NIR camera sensitivities, so these zero-valued predictions are censored. A censored regression was performed using the Python package [Tobit](https://github.com/jamesdj/tobit?tab=readme-ov-file) (Jensen, 2016) (Figure 1 – step 3). The output from the censored regression includes estimates for the y-intercept (**$\hat{\beta}_0$**), the slope (**$\hat{\beta}_1$**), and the standard deviation (**$\hat{\tau}$**) of the residual error distribution (**$\epsilon$**). Another important result at this step is the covariance matrix **$Var(\hat{\beta}_0,\hat{\beta}_1,\hat{\tau})$**, given by the Tobit package’s inverse Hessian matrix, which helps later in generating confidence bounds around the POD curve. 

Next, the POD model parameters are calculated (Figure 1 – step 4). These parameters are a transformed version of our regression parameters that define the POD curve. **$\hat{\mu}$** acts as the mean of the POD curve and is defined by the size at which there is 50% POD (i.e. **$a_{50}$** in Figure 2). **$\hat{\sigma}$** acts as the standard deviation, controlling how rapidly the POD curve rises from 0 to 1. The covariance of these parameters is also needed (Figure 1 – step 5), as it allows for the confidence bound to be calculated along the span of the POD curve. This covariance matrix, **$Var(\hat{\mu}, \hat{\sigma})$**, is a transformed version of the earlier covariance matrix, **$Var(\hat{\beta}_0,\hat{\beta}_1,\hat{\tau})$**, as a result of the Delta Method (Van der Vaart, 2000), which uses a Jacobian matrix, **$D$**, that contains the partial derivatives of the POD parameters **$(\hat{\mu}, \hat{\sigma})$**, with respect to the regression parameters **$(\hat{\beta}_0,\hat{\beta}_1,\hat{\tau})$** (U.S. Department of Defense, 2009). 

Next, the typical POD metrics are calculated, namely the **$a_{50}$** and **$a_{90/95}$** sizes (Figure 1 – step 6). The **$a_{50}$** size is equal to **$\hat{\mu}$**, as detailed previously. Less trivially, the **$a_{90/95}$** size first requires calculation of the **$a_{90}$** size (by adding the 90th-percentile of the standard normal, **$z_{90}$** (approx. 1.28), multiplied by **$\hat{\sigma}$**. The variance at **$a_{90}$** is calculated by using our POD parameter covariance matrix (**$Var(\hat{\mu}, \hat{\sigma})$**) with the Delta Method again, and this variance is used, along with the 95th-percentile of the standard normal, **$z_{95}$** (approx. 1.65), to calculate **$a_{90/95}$**. As a side note, the approach detailed here differs slightly from the method detailed in the POD manual (U.S. Department of Defense, 2009), as the Tobit package parameterises censored regression using the variance, **$\hat{\tau}^2**, in contrast to the log of the variance, **$log(\hat{\tau}^2)**, used in the manual and **$R$** implementation. Through a minor tweak in the Jacobian matrix, using **$\frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial \tau} = \frac{1}{\beta_1}$** rather than **$\frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial \log(\tau)} = \frac{\tau}{\beta_1}$**, the calculated outputs are identical.

Finally, A continuous POD curve can be generated by calculating the POD (and the upper 95th-percentile) across the relevant range of defect sizes (Figure 1 – step 7). The equation for the POD curve is defined by the equation below:

**$\text{POD}(a) = \Phi\left(\frac{a - \hat{\mu}}{\hat{\sigma}}\right)$**

where **$\Phi$** is the standard normal CDF, **$a$** is the defect size (in units of $mm^2$ in this work), and **$\hat{\mu}$** and **$\hat{\sigma}$** are the POD model parameters described earlier. The standard normal probability density function (PDF), **$\phi$**, is used to calculate the gradient vector, **$\nabla \Phi$**, at each defect size. The Delta method is used to approximate the variance of the POD curve at each defect size, allowing for calculation of the upper 95th-percentile confidence bound. The resultant POD curve is a CDF that describes each model’s POD as a function of defect size. When reporting results, defect sizes are expressed in the form of ECD rather than area, to provide a more intuitive physical scale.
